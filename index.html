Fair Trade — Study Sheet (Simple English)

Yes. This sheet answers the five questions you listed. Read the numbered sections below. Each section uses short bullet points for easy learning.

1. Description — What is this type of aid?

  • Fair Trade is a market-based form of development aid.
  • It uses labels and rules to make trade fairer for producers.
  • Key parts: a minimum price, a social premium, and certification rules.
  • Who gives it? Certification groups, ethical companies, NGOs, and consumers who buy fair products.
  • Who is the addressee? Small farmers, worker groups, and cooperatives in poor countries.

2. Advantages — What are the benefits?

  • Higher and more stable income for certified producers (minimum price).
  • Social premium money for community projects (schools, health, water).
  • Better working conditions and rules against child labor.
  • Encourages better farming methods and some environmental care.
  • Compared to direct aid: it links to markets and can be more lasting.
  • Who profits? Producers, their communities, and consumers who want ethical goods.
  • Positive effects: more money, local projects, stronger cooperatives, some job security.

3. Disadvantages / Problems

  • Certification costs and paperwork can be too hard for the poorest farmers.
  • Fair Trade is often a small niche market — not all products sell as fair trade.
  • Benefits do not always reach every family in a community (unequal distribution).
  • Risk of dependency on a few buyers or on export markets.
  • Some studies show mixed long-term effects — not always clear improvement for all.
  • Processes hampered: local smallholders may be left out; local markets can stay weak.

4. Example — One short case

  • Example: A coffee cooperative gets Fair Trade certification.
  • Advantages shown: they get a minimum price when world prices fall; they use the premium to build a school and buy a coffee dryer.
  • Problems shown: very small farms could not pay certification fees and stayed outside; some families did not get extra money.
  • Lesson: Fair Trade helps groups, but extra help (training, subsidies) is needed for the poorest.

5. Conclusion & Recommendation

  • Short answer: Fair Trade is useful and should continue, but with conditions.
  • It is more sustainable when combined with training, market access, and help to pay certification costs.
  • Do not rely on Fair Trade alone — add public programs and local development support.
  • Conditions for success: strong cooperatives, transparent use of premiums, support for the poorest, and market development.
  • Final note: Fair Trade can be part of long-term development if it is fair, inclusive, and supported by other measures.
